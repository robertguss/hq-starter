**Wendell Erdman Berry** (born August 5, 1934) is an American novelist, poet, essayist, [environmental activist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_activist "Environmental activist"), [cultural critic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_critic "Cultural critic"), and farmer.[[1]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-1) Closely identified with rural Kentucky, Berry developed many of his [agrarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agrarianism "Agrarianism") themes in the early essays of _The Gift of Good Land_ (1981) and _[The Unsettling of America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Unsettling\_of\_America "The Unsettling of America")_ (1977). His attention to the culture and economy of rural communities is also found in the novels and stories of [Port William](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Port_William_(Wendell_Berry) "Port William (Wendell Berry)"), such as _A Place on Earth_ (1967), _Jayber Crow_ (2000), and _That Distant Land_ (2004).

He is an elected member of the [Fellowship of Southern Writers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fellowship_of_Southern_Writers "Fellowship of Southern Writers"), a recipient of the [National Humanities Medal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Humanities_Medal "National Humanities Medal"), and the [Jefferson Lecturer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jefferson_Lecture "Jefferson Lecture") for 2012. He is also a 2013 Fellow of the [American Academy of Arts and Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences "American Academy of Arts and Sciences") and, since 2014, a member of the [American Academy of Arts and Letters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Letters "American Academy of Arts and Letters").[[2]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-2) Berry was named the recipient of the 2013 [Richard C. Holbrooke Distinguished Achievement Award](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_C._Holbrooke_Distinguished_Achievement_Award "Richard C. Holbrooke Distinguished Achievement Award").[[3]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-3) On January 28, 2015, he became the first living writer to be inducted into the Kentucky Writers Hall of Fame.[[4]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-4)

Berry was the first of four children to be born to John Marshall Berry, a lawyer and tobacco farmer in [Henry County, Kentucky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_County,_Kentucky "Henry County, Kentucky"), and Virginia Erdman Berry. The families of both parents had farmed in Henry County for at least five generations. Berry attended secondary school at [Millersburg Military Institute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millersburg_Military_Institute "Millersburg Military Institute") and then earned a B.A. (1956) and M.A. (1957) in English at the [University of Kentucky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Kentucky "University of Kentucky").[[5]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-PWNS-2018-5): 990–991  In 1956, at the University of Kentucky he met another Kentucky writer-to-be, [Gurney Norman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gurney_Norman "Gurney Norman").[[6]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-6) He completed his M.A. and married Tanya Amyx in 1957. In 1958, he attended [Stanford University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stanford_University "Stanford University")'s creative writing program as a [Wallace Stegner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wallace_Stegner "Wallace Stegner") Fellow, studying under Stegner in a seminar that included [Larry McMurtry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Larry_McMurtry "Larry McMurtry"), [Robert Stone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Stone_(novelist) "Robert Stone (novelist)"), [Ernest Gaines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernest_Gaines "Ernest Gaines"), [Tillie Olsen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tillie_Olsen "Tillie Olsen"), and [Ken Kesey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ken_Kesey "Ken Kesey").[[7]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-Angyal-1995-8): 139  Berry's first novel, _Nathan Coulter_, was published in April 1960.

A [Guggenheim Fellowship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Simon_Guggenheim_Memorial_Foundation "John Simon Guggenheim Memorial Foundation") took Berry and his family to Italy and France in 1961, where he came to know [Wallace Fowlie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wallace_Fowlie "Wallace Fowlie"), critic and translator of French literature. From 1962 to 1964, he taught English at [New York University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_University "New York University")'s University Heights campus in [the Bronx](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Bronx "The Bronx"). In 1964, he began teaching creative writing at the [University of Kentucky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Kentucky "University of Kentucky"), from which he resigned in 1977.[[8]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-Angyal-1995-8) During this time in [Lexington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lexington,_Kentucky "Lexington, Kentucky"), he came to know author [Guy Davenport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guy_Davenport "Guy Davenport"), as well as author and monk [Thomas Merton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Merton "Thomas Merton") and photographer [Ralph Eugene Meatyard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ralph_Eugene_Meatyard "Ralph Eugene Meatyard").[[9]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-9)

On July 4, 1965, Berry, his wife, and his two children moved to Lane's Landing, a 12-acre farm (4.9 ha) that he had purchased, and began growing corn and small grains on what eventually became a homestead of about 117 acres (47 ha).[[5]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-PWNS-2018-5): 994  They bought their first flock of seven Border Cheviot sheep in 1978.[[5]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-PWNS-2018-5): 998  Lane's Landing is in [Henry County](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_County,_Kentucky "Henry County, Kentucky") in north central Kentucky near [Port Royal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Port_Royal,_Kentucky "Port Royal, Kentucky"), and his parents' birthplaces, and is on the western bank of the [Kentucky River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kentucky_River "Kentucky River"), not far from where it flows into the [Ohio River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ohio_River "Ohio River"). Berry has farmed, resided, and written at Lane's Landing ever since. He has written about his early experiences on the land and about his decision to return to it in essays such as "The Long-Legged House" and "A Native Hill".[[10]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-10)

From 1977 until 1980, he edited and wrote for [Rodale, Inc.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rodale,_Inc. "Rodale, Inc.") in [Emmaus, Pennsylvania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emmaus,_Pennsylvania "Emmaus, Pennsylvania"), including for its publications _Organic Gardening and Farming_ and _The New Farm_.[[5]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-PWNS-2018-5): 998  From 1987 to 1993, he returned to the English Department of the University of Kentucky.[[8]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-Angyal-1995-8)[[11]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-11) Berry has written at least twenty-five books (or [chapbooks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chapbook "Chapbook")) of poems, twenty-four volumes of essays, and fifteen novels and short story collections. His writing is grounded in the notion that one's work ought to be rooted in and responsive to one's place.

Berry delivered "A Statement Against the War in Vietnam" during the Kentucky Conference on the War and the Draft on February 10, 1968, at the [University of Kentucky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Kentucky "University of Kentucky") in Lexington:[[12]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-12)

> We seek to preserve peace by fighting a war, or to advance freedom by subsidizing dictatorships, or to 'win the hearts and minds of the people' by poisoning their crops and burning their villages and confining them in concentration camps; we seek to uphold the 'truth' of our cause with lies, or to answer conscientious dissent with threats and slurs and intimidations. . . . I have come to the realization that I can no longer imagine a war that I would believe to be either useful or necessary. I would be against any war.[[13]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-13)

He debated former [Secretary of Agriculture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secretary_of_Agriculture "Secretary of Agriculture")[Earl Butz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earl_Butz "Earl Butz") at [Manchester University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manchester_University_(Indiana) "Manchester University (Indiana)") in North Manchester, Indiana in November 1977.[[14]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-14) In this debate Berry defended the longstanding structure of small family farms and rural communities that were being replaced by what Butz saw as the achievements of [industrial agriculture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrial_agriculture "Industrial agriculture"). "My basic assumption when talking about agriculture is that there's more to it than just agriculture. That you can't disconnect one part of a society from all the other parts and just look at the results and that alone."[[15]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-15)[[16]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-16)

On June 3, 1979, Berry engaged in nonviolent [civil disobedience](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_disobedience "Civil disobedience") against the construction of a [nuclear power plant at Marble Hill, Indiana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marble_Hill_Nuclear_Power_Plant "Marble Hill Nuclear Power Plant"). He describes "this nearly eventless event" and expands upon his reasons for it in the essay "The Reactor and the Garden."[[17]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-17)

On February 9, 2003, Berry's essay titled "A Citizen's Response to the National Security Strategy of the United States" was published as a full-page advertisement in _The New York Times_. Berry opened the essay—a critique of the [George W. Bush](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_W._Bush "George W. Bush") administration's [post-9/11](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Post-9/11 "Post-9/11") international strategy[[18]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-18)—by asserting that "The new National Security Strategy published by the White House in September 2002, if carried out, would amount to a radical revision of the political character of our nation."[[19]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-19)[_[non-primary source needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:No\_original\_research#Primary,\_secondary\_and\_tertiary\_sources "Wikipedia:No original research")_]

On January 4, 2009, Berry and [Wes Jackson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wes_Jackson "Wes Jackson"), president of [The Land Institute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Land_Institute "The Land Institute"), published an op-ed article in _The New York Times_ titled "A 50-Year Farm Bill."[[20]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-nytimes2009-20) In July 2009 Berry, Jackson and [Fred Kirschenmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fred_Kirschenmann "Fred Kirschenmann"), of The [Leopold Center for Sustainable Agriculture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leopold_Center_for_Sustainable_Agriculture "Leopold Center for Sustainable Agriculture"), gathered in Washington DC to promote this idea.[[21]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-21) Berry and Jackson wrote, "We need a 50-year farm bill that addresses forthrightly the problems of soil loss and degradation, toxic pollution, fossil-fuel dependency and the destruction of rural communities."[[20]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-nytimes2009-20)

Also in January 2009, Berry released a statement against the death penalty, which began, "As I am made deeply uncomfortable by the taking of a human life before birth, I am also made deeply uncomfortable by the taking of a human life after birth."[[22]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-22) And in November 2009, Berry and 38 other writers from Kentucky wrote to [Gov. Steve Beshear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steve_Beshear "Steve Beshear") and [Attorney General Jack Conway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jack_Conway_(politician) "Jack Conway (politician)") asking them to impose a moratorium on the death penalty in that state.[[23]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-23)

On March 2, 2009, Berry joined over 2,000 others in non-violently blocking the gates to a coal-fired power plant in Washington, D.C. No one was arrested.[[24]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-24)

On May 22, 2009, Berry, at a listening session in Louisville, spoke against the [National Animal Identification System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Animal_Identification_System "National Animal Identification System") (NAIS).[[25]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-25) He said, "If you impose this program on the small farmers, who are already overburdened, you're going to have to send the police for me. I'm 75 years old. I've about completed my responsibilities to my family. I'll lose very little in going to jail in opposition to your program – and I'll have to do it. Because I will be, in every way that I can conceive of, a non-cooperator."[[26]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-26)

In October 2009, Berry combined with "the Berea-based Kentucky Environmental Foundation (KEF), along with several other non-profit organizations and rural electric co-op members" to petition against and protest the construction of a coal-burning power plant in [Clark County, Kentucky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clark_County,_Kentucky "Clark County, Kentucky").[[27]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-27) On February 28, 2011, the [Kentucky Public Service Commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kentucky_Public_Service_Commission "Kentucky Public Service Commission") approved the cancellation of this power plant.[[28]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-28)

On December 20, 2009, due to the University of Kentucky's close association with coal interests in the state, Berry removed his papers from the university. He explained to the _[Lexington Herald-Leader](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lexington\_Herald-Leader "Lexington Herald-Leader")_, "I don't think the University of Kentucky can be so ostentatiously friendly to the coal industry … and still be a friend to me and the interests for which I have stood for the last 45 years. … If they love the coal industry that much, I have to cancel my friendship."[[29]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-29) In August 2012, the papers were donated to [The Kentucky Historical Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Kentucky_Historical_Society "The Kentucky Historical Society") in Frankfort.[[30]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-30)

On September 28, 2010, Berry participated in a rally in Louisville during an EPA hearing on how to manage [coal ash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coal_ash "Coal ash"). Berry said, "The EPA knows that coal ash is poison. We ask it only to believe in its own findings on this issue, and do its duty."[[31]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-31)

Berry, with 14 other protesters, spent the weekend of February 12, 2011, locked in the Kentucky governor's office to demand an end to [mountaintop removal coal mining](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mountaintop_removal_coal_mining "Mountaintop removal coal mining"). He was part of the environmental group [Kentuckians for the Commonwealth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kentuckians_for_the_Commonwealth "Kentuckians for the Commonwealth") that began their sit-in on Friday and left at midday Monday to join about 1,000 others in a mass outdoor rally.[[32]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-32)[[33]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-33)

In 2011, the Berry Center was established at [New Castle, Kentucky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Castle,_Kentucky "New Castle, Kentucky"), "for the purpose of bringing focus, knowledge and cohesiveness to the work of changing our ruinous industrial agriculture system into a system and culture that uses nature as the standard, accepts no permanent damage to the ecosphere, and takes into consideration human health in local communities."[[34]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-34)

In June 2020, Wendell Berry endorsed [Mike Broihier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mike_Broihier "Mike Broihier") in the Democratic primary for Kentucky's [2020 U.S. Senate election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2020_U.S._Senate_election_in_Kentucky "2020 U.S. Senate election in Kentucky").[[35]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-35)

In July 2020, Wendell Berry and his wife Tanya Amyx Berry sued the University of Kentucky to prevent the removal of a mural that has been criticized for being "racially offensive."[[36]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-36) The mural was commissioned in the 1930s and was done by [Ann Rice O'Hanlon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ann_Rice_O%27Hanlon "Ann Rice O'Hanlon"), a relative of Tanya Amyx Berry.[[37]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-37)

In August 2022, at a public hearing of the Henry County, Kentucky planning commission, Wendell Berry spoke against re-zoning agricultural land to allow Angel's Envy distillery to develop the property "for bourbon-barrel storage and the development of an agritourism destination." Despite the testimony by Berry and others, the planning commission granted the re-zoning request.[[38]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-38)[[39]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-39)

Berry's [nonfiction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonfiction "Nonfiction") serves as an extended conversation about the life he values. According to him, the good life includes sustainable agriculture,[[40]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-40)[appropriate technologies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Appropriate_technologies "Appropriate technologies"),[[41]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-41) healthy rural [communities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communities "Communities"),[[42]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-42) connection to place,[[43]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-43) the pleasures of good food,[[44]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-44)[animal husbandry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal_husbandry "Animal husbandry"),[[45]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-45) good work,[[46]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-46) local economics,[[47]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-47) the [miracle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miracle "Miracle") of life,[[48]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-48) fidelity,[[49]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-49)[frugality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frugality "Frugality"),[[50]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-50) reverence,[[51]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-51) and the interconnectedness of life.[[52]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-52) The threats Berry finds to this good [simple life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_life "Simple life") include: [industrial farming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrial_farming "Industrial farming") and the industrialization of life,[[53]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-53) ignorance,[[54]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-54) hubris,[[55]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-55) greed,[[56]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-56) violence against others and against the [natural world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nature "Nature"),[[57]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-57) the eroding [topsoil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topsoil "Topsoil") in the United States,[[58]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-58)[global economics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Globalization "Globalization"),[[59]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-59) and [environmental destruction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_environment "Natural environment").[[60]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-60) As a prominent defender of agrarian values, Berry's appreciation for traditional farming techniques,[[61]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-61) such as those of the [Amish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amish "Amish"), grew in the 1970s, due in part to exchanges with _Draft Horse Journal_ publisher Maurice Telleen.[[62]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-62) Berry has long been friendly to and supportive of [Wes Jackson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wes_Jackson "Wes Jackson"), believing that Jackson's agricultural research at The [Land Institute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Land_Institute "Land Institute") lives out the promise of "solving for pattern" and using "nature as model."

Jedediah Britton-Purdy has considered many of Berry's major themes and concerns:

> Over the years, he has called himself an agrarian, a pacifist, and a Christian—albeit of an eccentric kind. He has written against all forms of violence and destruction—of land, communities, and human beings—and argued that the modern American way of life is a skein of violence. He is an anti-capitalist moralist and a writer of praise for what he admires: the quiet, mostly uncelebrated labor and affection that keep the world whole and might still redeem it. He is also an acerbic critic of what he dislikes, particularly modern individualism, and his emphasis on family and marriage and his ambivalence toward abortion mark him as an outsider to the left.[[63]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-63)

The concept of "_Solving for pattern_", coined by Berry in his essay of the same title, is the process of finding solutions that solve multiple problems, while minimizing the creation of new problems.[[64]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-64) The essay was originally published in the [Rodale, Inc.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rodale,_Inc. "Rodale, Inc.") periodical _The New Farm_. Though Berry's use of the phrase was in direct reference to agriculture, it has since come to enjoy broader use throughout the design community.[[65]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-65)[[66]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-66)

Berry, who describes himself as "a person who takes the Gospel seriously,"[[67]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-67) has criticized Christian organizations for failing to challenge cultural complacency about [environmental degradation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_degradation "Environmental degradation"),[[68]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-68)[[69]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-69) and has shown a willingness to criticize what he perceives as the arrogance of some Christians.[[70]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-70) He is an advocate of [Christian pacifism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christian_pacifism "Christian pacifism"), as shown in his book _Blessed Are the Peacemakers: [Christ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christ "Christ")'s Teachings About Love, Compassion and Forgiveness_ (2005).

Berry's core ideas, and in particular his poem "Sabbaths III, 1989 (Santa Clara Valley)," guided the 2007 documentary feature film _The Unforeseen_, produced by [Terrence Malick](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terrence_Malick "Terrence Malick") and [Robert Redford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Redford "Robert Redford").[[71]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-71)[[72]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-72) In the film Berry narrates his own poem.[[73]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-73) Director Laura Dunn went on to make the 2016 documentary feature _[Look & See: A Portrait of Wendell Berry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Look\_%26\_See:\_A\_Portrait\_of\_Wendell\_Berry "Look & See: A Portrait of Wendell Berry")_, again produced by Malick and Redford.[[74]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-74)

Berry's [lyric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lyric_poetry "Lyric poetry") poetry often appears as a contemporary [eclogue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eclogue "Eclogue"), [pastoral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pastoral "Pastoral"), or [elegy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elegy "Elegy"); but he also composes dramatic and historical [narratives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Narrative_poetry "Narrative poetry") (such as "Bringer of Water"[[75]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-75) and "July, 1773",[[76]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-76) respectively) and occasional and discursive poems ("Against the War in Vietnam"[[77]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-77) and "Some Further Words",[[78]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-78) respectively).

Berry's first published poetry book consisted of a single poem, the elegiac _November Twenty Six Nineteen Hundred Sixty Three_ (1964), initiated and illustrated by [Ben Shahn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ben_Shahn "Ben Shahn"), commemorating the death of [John F. Kennedy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_F._Kennedy "John F. Kennedy"). It begins,

We know

 The winter earth

 Upon the body

 Of the young

President,

And the early dark

Falling;

and continues through ten more stanzas (each propelled by the [anaphora](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaphora_(rhetoric) "Anaphora (rhetoric)") of "We know"). The elegiac here and elsewhere, according to Triggs, enables Berry to characterize the connections "that link past and future generations through their common working of the land."[[79]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-79)

The first full-length collection, _The Broken Ground_ (1964), develops many of Berry's fundamental concerns: "the cycle of life and death, responsiveness to place, pastoral subject matter, and recurring images of the Kentucky River and the hill farms of north-central Kentucky."[[8]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-Angyal-1995-8): 119

According to Angyal, "There is little modernist formalism or postmodernist experimentation in [Berry's] verse."[[8]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-Angyal-1995-8): 116  A commitment to the reality and primacy of the actual world stands behind these two rejections. In "Notes: Unspecializing Poetry," Berry writes, "Devotion to order that is not poetical prevents the specialization of poetry."[[80]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-80) He goes on to note, "Nothing exists for its own sake, but for a harmony greater than itself which includes it. A work of art, which accepts this condition, and exists upon its terms, honors the Creation, and so becomes a part of it."[[81]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-81)

[Lionel Basney](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lionel_Basney "Lionel Basney") placed Berry's poetry within a tradition of [didactic poetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Didactic_poetry "Didactic poetry") that stretches back to [Horace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horace "Horace"): "To say that Berry's poetry can be didactic, then, means that it envisions a specific wisdom, and also the traditional sense of art and culture that gives art the task of teaching this wisdom."[[82]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-82)

For Berry, poetry exists "at the center of a complex reminding"[[83]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-83) Both the poet and the reader are reminded of the poem's crafted language, of the poem's formal literary antecedents, of "what is remembered or ought to be remembered," and of "the formal integrity of other works, creatures and structures of the world."[[84]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-84)

From 1979 to the present Berry has been writing what he calls "[Sabbath](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sabbath "Sabbath") poems." They were first collected in _A Timbered Choir: The Sabbath Poems 1979–1997_. This was followed by Sabbaths from 1998 to 2004 in _Given: New Poems_; and those from 2005 to 2008 are in _Leavings_. All Sabbath poems through 2012 are published in _This Day: New and Collected Sabbath Poems 1979 – 2012_. _Sabbaths 2013_ has been published by [Larkspur Press](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Larkspur_Press "Larkspur Press"). _A Small Porch_ contains nine Sabbath poems from 2014 and sixteen from 2015. One Sabbath poem, "What Passes, What Remains" (VIII from 2016), is published as the epilogue in _The Art of Loading Brush_. That poem, along with fourteen others, can also be found in _Sabbaths 2016_, published by Larkspur Press.

The poems are motivated by Berry's longtime habit of walking out onto the land on Sunday mornings. As he puts it, "I go free from the tasks and intentions of my workdays, and so my mind becomes hospitable to unintended thoughts: to what I am very willing to call inspiration."[[85]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-85) He writes in a poem from 1979,

The bell calls in the town 

 Where forebears cleared the shaded land

 And brought high daylight down

 To shine on field and trodden road.

 I hear, but understand

 Contrarily, and walk into the woods.

 I leave labor and load,

 Take up a different story.

 I keep an inventory

 Of wonders and of uncommercial goods.[[86]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-86)

The Sabbath poems have been described as "written from a particular place and on particular Sabbaths, and so should be read as part of a spiritual practice and as poems, in some sense, devoted to dwelling, to living thoughtfully in one place."[[87]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-87) Oehlschlaeger links Berry's project to a key observation by [Henry David Thoreau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_David_Thoreau "Henry David Thoreau"),

> As Thoreau continues in '[Life Without Principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life_Without_Principle "Life Without Principle"),' he notes the constant busyness of Americans, so engaged in 'infinite bustle' that 'there is no sabbath.' And he notes later that 'there is nothing, not even crime, more opposed to poetry, to philosophy, ay, to life itself, than this incessant business.' The logic is clear: destruction of the Sabbath is contrary to 'life itself.' That, I suggest, is the context in which we should read the Sabbath poems that Berry has been writing for nearly the last thirty years.[[88]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-88)

Berry's fiction to date consists of eight novels and fifty-seven short stories—all of which are collected in _That Distant Land_ (2004), _A Place in Time_ (2012) and _How It Went_ (2022)—and one verse drama which, when read as a whole, form a chronicle of the fictional small Kentucky town of [Port William](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Port_William_(Wendell_Berry) "Port William (Wendell Berry)"). Because of his long-term, ongoing exploration of the life of an imagined place, Berry has been compared to [William Faulkner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Faulkner "William Faulkner").[[89]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-89) Yet, although Port William is no stranger to murder, suicide, alcoholism, marital discord, and the full range of losses that touch human lives, it lacks the extremes of characterization and plot development that are found in much of Faulkner. Hence Berry is sometimes described as working in an idealized, pastoral, or nostalgic mode, a characterization of his work which he resists: "If your work includes a criticism of history, which mine certainly does, you can't be accused of wanting to go back to something, because you're saying that what we were wasn't good enough."[[90]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-90)[[91]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-91)

The effect of profound shifts in the agricultural practices of the United States and the disappearance of traditional agrarian life[[92]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-92) are two of the major concerns of the Port William fiction, though these themes are often only a background or subtext to the stories themselves. The Port William fiction attempts to portray, on a local scale, what "a human economy … conducted with reverence"[[93]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-93) looked like in the past—and what civic, domestic, and personal virtues might be elicited by such an economy, were it pursued today. Social as well as seasonal changes mark the passage of time. The Port William stories allow Berry to explore the human dimensions of the decline of the family farm and farm community under the influence of expanding post-[World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II")[agribusiness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agribusiness "Agribusiness"). But these works rarely fall into simple didacticism, and are never merely tales of decline. Each is grounded in a realistic depiction of character and community. In _A Place on Earth_ (1967), for example, farmer Mat Feltner comes to terms with the loss of his only son, Virgil. In the course of the novel, we see how not only Mat but the entire community wrestles with the acute costs of World War II.

Berry's fiction also allows him to explore the literal and metaphorical implications of [marriage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marriage "Marriage") as that which binds individuals, families, and communities to each other, and to Nature itself. Yet not all of Port William is happily or conventionally married. "Old Jack" Beechum struggles with significant incompatibilities with his wife, and with a brief yet fulfilling extramarital affair. The barber Jayber Crow lives with a forlorn, secret, and unrequited love for a woman he believes himself "mentally" married to, though she knows nothing about it. Burley Coulter never formalizes his bond with Kate Helen Branch, the mother of his son. Yet, each of these men finds himself firmly bound up in the community, the "membership," of Port William.

Of his fictional project, Berry has written: "I have made the imagined town of Port William, its neighborhood and membership, in an attempt to honor the actual place where I have lived. By means of the imagined place, over the last fifty years, I have learned to see my native landscape and neighborhood as a place unique in the world, a work of God, possessed of an inherent sanctity that mocks any human valuation that can be put upon it."[[94]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-94) Elsewhere, Berry has said, "The only thing I try to accomplish in fiction is to show how people act when they love each other."[[95]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-95) The novels and stories can be read in any order.

In January 2018, the [Library of America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Library_of_America "Library of America") published a volume of Berry's fiction—the first of a projected four volumes of his writing. _Wendell Berry: Port William Novels & Stories (The Civil War to World War II)_ contains four novels and twenty-three short stories in chronological order according to the stories' events.[[96]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-96) Berry is one of very few living writers currently featured in the Library of America catalog.[[97]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-97)

### _Nathan Coulter_ (1960)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Wendell_Berry&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Nathan Coulter (1960)")]

In Berry's first novel, young Nathan "comes of age" through dealing with the death of his mother, the depression of his father, Jarrat, the rugged companionship of his brother Tom, and the mischief of his uncle Burley. _Kirkus Review_ concludes, "A sensitive adolescent theme is handled rather poetically, but so uniform in tone that no drama is generated and no sense of time passing is felt."[[98]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-98) John Ditsky finds William Faulkner's influence in _Nathan Coulter_, but notes, "Not only does the work avoid the pitfalls encountered by Faulkner's initial attempts to escape his postage stamp of native soil, but Nathan Coulter also seems a wise attempt to get that autobiographical first novel out of one's system, and to do so [with] honesty."[[99]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-99)

### _A Place on Earth_ (1967/1983)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Wendell_Berry&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: A Place on Earth (1967/1983)")]

Set in the critical year of 1945, this novel focuses on farmer Mat Feltner's struggle over the news that his son Virgil has been listed as missing in action while also telling multiple tales of the lives of other Port William residents, such as Burley Coulter, Jack Beechum, Ernest Finley, Ida and Gideon Crop. Reprinting by North Point Press in 1983 allowed Berry to radically revise the novel,[[100]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-100) removing almost a third of its original length. Jeffrey Bilbro believes that these substantial changes marked growth in Berry's approach. "In Berry's revised edition, his technique caught up with his subject. He allows us, as readers, to participate in the ignorance of his characters, and in doing so, we may be able to understand more fully the painful difficulty of choosing fidelity to the natural order while living in the midst of mystery."[[101]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-101)

### _The Memory of Old Jack_ (1974)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Wendell_Berry&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: The Memory of Old Jack  (1974)")]

This third novel of Port William begins with Jack Beechum as a very old man in 1952 and continues back into his youth and maturity to uncover his life and work as a dedicated farmer, conflicted husband, and living link to past generations. The story ranges from the Civil War to just past World War II. Josh Hurst comments on Berry's ability to avoid certain narrative pitfalls, "Jack's story could be presented us either as heroic ballad or as cautionary [tale]—and there is much in his life to support both admiration and gentle tisk-tisking—but the gift of this book is how it allows a man's memories to wash over us as though unshaped by narrative or conscious editorializing."[[102]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-102)

In Berry's fourth novel, an adult Andy Catlett wanders through San Francisco remembering, but feeling alienated from, his native Port William. He struggles to come to terms with himself, his marriage, his farm, and the distorted values of American society. Of Berry's vision here, Charles Solomon writes, "Wendell Berry contrasts modern American agribusiness—which he depicts as an artificial conglomeration of sterile flow charts, debts and mechanization—with the older ideal of farming as a nurturing way of life."[[103]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-103) But along these lines, Bruce Bawer finds a problem with the novel, "Here, for the first time in a Port William novel, Berry seems more interested in communicating opinions than in portraying sympathetic characters in plausible situations; the opening episode, set at a conference on agricultural policy, paints the ideological conflict between Andy and his adversaries in broad, unsubtle strokes."[[104]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-104)

### _A World Lost_ (1996)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Wendell_Berry&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: A World Lost (1996)")]

Young Andy Catlett's uncle Andrew had been murdered back in 1944, and now an adult Andy is reconstructing the event and its aftermath. "Looking back with a mixture of a young boy's incomprehension and an older man's nostalgia, Andy evokes the past not as a narrative but as a series of disembodied fragments in the flow of time."[[105]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-105) In this fifth novel of Port William, Berry considers the violence of men and its impact on the family and community that must come to terms with it. "Berry shows us the psychic costs of misplaced family pride and social rigidity, and yet he also celebrates the benevolent blessing of familial love. This is simple, soul-satisfying storytelling, augmented by understated humor and quiet insight."[[106]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-106)

Port William's barber recounts his life's journey in Berry's sixth novel. Jayber's early life as an orphan near Port William is followed by studies towards a possible vocation to Church ministry. A questioning mind, however, sends him in other directions until he finds himself back in Port William with an ever-growing commitment to that place and its people. As _Publishers Weekly_ notes, "Crow's life, which begins as WWI is about to erupt, is emblematic of a century of upheaval, and Berry's anecdotal and episodic tale sounds a challenge to contemporary notions of progress. It is to Berry's credit that a novel so freighted with ideas and ideology manages to project such warmth and luminosity."[[107]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-107)

### _Hannah Coulter_ (2004)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Wendell_Berry&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Hannah Coulter (2004)")]

Berry's seventh novel presents a concise vision of Port William's "membership." The story encompasses Hannah's life, including the [Great Depression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Depression "Great Depression"), [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"), the postwar industrialization of agriculture, the flight of youth to urban employment, and the consequent remoteness of grandchildren. The tale is told in the voice of an old woman twice widowed, who has experienced much loss yet has never been defeated. Somehow, lying at the center of her strength is the "membership"—the fact that people care for each other and, even in absence, hold each other in a kind of presence. All in all, _Hannah Coulter_ embodies many of the themes of Berry's Port William saga.

### _Andy Catlett: Early Travels_ (2006)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Wendell_Berry&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Andy Catlett: Early Travels (2006)")]

Andy Catlett, age nine, makes his first solo journey to visit with both sets of grandparents in Port William. _The New York Times_ reviewer notes, "What the grown-up Andy recalls of that experience is transformed into 'a sort of homage' to a now-vanished world. Title characters from Berry's earlier Port William volumes — Jayber Crow, Old Jack, Hannah Coulter — appear here in affectionate cameos as the adult Andy, echoing Wordsworth, observes that 'in my memory, all who were there ... seem now to be gathered into a love that is at once a boy's and an aging man's.'"[[108]](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendell_Berry#cite_note-108)

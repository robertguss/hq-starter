## The SLC Approach: Building Products That Customers Love

![Image 35: Picture of Mark Donnigan](https://growthstage.marketing/wp-content/uploads/2020/10/Mark-Donnigan-Headshot-black-shirt-bw-300x300.jpg)

### Mark Donnigan

|

October 7, 2024

> As a technical founder, you’ve likely encountered the Minimum Viable Product (MVP) concept. It’s been the go-to strategy for startups looking to launch quickly and iterate based on user feedback.
> 
> 
> But what if there’s a better way?
> 
> 
> Enter the SLC approach: **Simple**, **Lovable**, and **Complete**.
> 
> 
> This article is for startup founders who want to create products that meet basic needs and delight users from day one. The SLC approach can lead to higher customer satisfaction, better retention rates, and a faster path to product-market fit.

### The Problem with MVPs

The MVP concept, popularized by Eric Ries in “The Lean Startup,” has been widely misinterpreted. Many founders take **“minimum”** to mean **“bare bones”** and **“viable”** to mean **“barely functional.”** This misunderstanding leads to incomplete, buggy, and frustrating products for users.

Consider the case of a social media scheduling tool that launches with just the ability to post to one platform. It’s “viable” because it does something, but it’s far from lovable or complete for most social media managers who need to post across multiple platforms.

### SLC vs. MVP: A Comparative Analysis

While MVP focuses on speed to market and learning, SLC prioritizes user satisfaction and retention. MVPs often lead to high initial interest but poor retention, whereas SLC products might take longer to build but tend to have better retention and word-of-mouth growth.

MVP is still valuable when the problem space is poorly understood or when testing radically new ideas. However, SLC will likely yield better results for most startups with known customer pain points in established markets.

**Criteria****MVP****SLC**
**Focus**Basic, essential features Complete, enjoyable product
**User Experience**Functional, often basic Polished, user-centric
**Development Speed**Faster to market Slower, more detailed
**Iteration Requirement**Frequent, based on feedback Fewer, product feels complete
**Risk**Higher, unfinished product Lower, engaging from launch
**Target Audience**Early adopters General users
**Brand Perception**Could seem rushed Stronger, polished reputation
**Market Differentiation**Quick, but basic entry Immediate, engaging differentiation

While MVP focuses on **speed and minimalism**, SLC emphasizes launching a product that is functional, enjoyable, and fully meets user needs from the outset. This approach will create initial solid user engagement and long-term success. SLC’s focus on the **user experience and completeness** helps build stronger brand loyalty and reduces the need for constant updates​.

### Unpacking SLC: Simple

In the SLC framework, **“Simple”** doesn’t mean feature-poor. Instead, it means focused and intuitive. A simple product does one thing (or a small set of related things) exceptionally well. It’s easy to understand and use, with a clear value proposition.

Take the example of a popular file-syncing service. While file syncing wasn’t new, this company made it simple. Install a folder on your computer; anything you put in it syncs automatically. There is no complicated setup or confusing interface. Just drag and drop, and you’re done. This simplicity was key to its rapid adoption and growth.

To create a simple product:

*   Identify your core value proposition
*   Ruthlessly prioritize features
*   Focus on intuitive design and user experience
*   Eliminate unnecessary complexity

### Unpacking SLC: Lovable

The SLC approach shines in **“lovable.”** A lovable product delights users, solving their problems almost magically. It doesn’t have to be perfect, but it should spark joy or relief.

Lovability can come from various sources:

*   Exceptional design and user interface
*   Solving a painful problem in a novel way
*   Surprising and delighting users with thoughtful features
*   Outstanding customer service

A team communication tool is a great example of a lovable product. While it’s “just” a messaging app, its delightful interface, fun integrations (like custom emojis), and powerful search functionality made users fall in love with it, driving rapid adoption within organizations.

To make your product lovable:

*   Deeply understand your users’ pain points and desires
*   Inject personality into your product
*   Pay attention to details that enhance the user experience
*   Continually gather and act on user feedback

### Unpacking SLC: Complete

**“Complete”** in the SLC context means that your product fully delivers on its core promise. It doesn’t need every feature imaginable, but it should solve the main problem it set out to address without major gaps.

For instance, when a popular video conferencing tool launched, it provided a complete solution. While it lacked some of the advanced features of its competitors, it delivered on its core promise: easy, reliable video calls. Users could set up and join meetings with minimal friction, and the video and audio quality were consistently good. This is how LiveSwitch competes alongside competitors many times their size.

To ensure your product is complete:

*   Clearly define your product’s core promise
*   Identify the minimum feature set needed to fulfill that promise
*   Thoroughly test to ensure reliability and performance
*   Provide necessary onboarding and support

### Implementing SLC in Your Startup

Adopting the SLC approach requires a shift in mindset and process:

1.       1.   Start with extensive customer research to understand your target users’ needs and pain points.
    2.   Define your product’s core promise and the minimum feature set needed to deliver on it.
    3.   Focus on design and user experience from the outset rather than as an afterthought.
    4.   Build in stages, but wait to release until you have something simple, lovable, and complete.
    5.   Gather feedback continuously and iterate rapidly, but always maintain the SLC principles.

### Measuring Success with SLC

Key metrics for SLC products include:

*   User retention rates
*   Net Promoter Score (NPS)
*   Time to value (how quickly users derive benefit)
*   Feature adoption rates
*   Qualitative feedback (look for emotional responses)

Success with an SLC product often manifests as high retention rates, strong word-of-mouth growth, and passionate user testimonials.

### Conclusion

The SLC approach offers a compelling alternative to the traditional MVP model. By focusing on creating simple, lovable, and complete products, startups can increase their chances of finding product-market fit and building a loyal customer base.

While it may require more upfront investment in time and resources, the payoff regarding customer satisfaction, retention, and word-of-mouth growth can be significant. In a world where users are bombarded with new products daily, creating something truly simple, lovable, and complete can be the key to standing out and succeeding.

As you embark on your next product development journey, consider embracing the SLC mindset. Your users – and your business – will thank you for it.

To implement the SLC approach successfully:

1.       1.   Conduct thorough customer research to understand pain points and desires.
    2.   Define your product’s core promise and the minimum features needed to fulfill it.
    3.   Focus on creating an intuitive, delightful user experience from the start.
    4.   Build in stages, but wait to launch until your product is simple, lovable, and complete.
    5.   Continuously gather feedback and iterate, always maintaining the SLC principles.
    6.   Measure success through retention rates, NPS, and qualitative feedback.

Remember, the goal is to build a product and create something your customers will love and champion. With the SLC approach, you’re well on your way to achieving that goal.

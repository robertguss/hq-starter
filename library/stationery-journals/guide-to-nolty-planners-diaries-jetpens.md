Paper Quality

NOLTY paper is sourced from responsibly managed forests and is certified by the Forest Stewardship Council (FSC). It’s lightweight to keep [NOLTY Planners](https://www.jetpens.com/NOLTY-Planners/ct/7324?f=ac22043281c97821&sa=product_date_added_dt) as slim as possible. However, NOLTY paper is also very durable to make sure it doesn’t tear when you open and close the planners countless times over the course of a year.

There are two kinds of paper used in NOLTY planners: NOLTY White and and NOLTY Cream. Both have yellow tones, which reflect less light than pure white paper. Please note that both papers are labeled “Ivory” on the JetPens website, but NOLTY Cream paper is darker than NOLTY White.

The yellow-tinged, white paper is similar in feel to 60 gsm paper, while the cream paper feels closer to 65 gsm. We compared them to similar papers and found that they were both heavier than [Tomoe River S paper](https://www.jetpens.com/blog/The-Best-Pens-Accessories-for-Tomoe-River-S-Paper/pt/992) and lighter than [Midori MD paper](https://www.jetpens.com/blog/Midori-MD-Notebooks-A-Comprehensive-Guide/pt/332). However, please note that these are our best guesses on paper weights; NOLTY’s paper details are proprietary.

NOLTY History

[NOLTY](https://www.jetpens.com/NOLTY/bd/441) began in 1949 with the creation of the “efficiency notebook” (能率手帳) by Iwao Ohno, the director of the Japan Management Association.

In the years after NOLTY was launched, the company worked hard to change the image of planners as things people received as inexpensive gifts. They refined the books’ designs and began using paper specifically designed for planners. Soon, the efficiency notebook became a prime example of a planner that was worth paying for.

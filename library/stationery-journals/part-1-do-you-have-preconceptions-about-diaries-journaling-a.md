### I try to follow rules like 

writing entries with

3 sentences, 

or 5 sentences, 

and I still can’t stick with it.

![Image 9: 古賀さんの答え](https://www.1101.com/store/techo/tpr/contents/images/4pxgotad8_1734940677875_kogasan_answer_en.png)

![Image 10: The more rules you set, the more trouble some it feels.](https://www.1101.com/store/techo/tpr/contents/images/4pxgotad8_1735022752653_q1_answer_pc_2.png)

I say this to everyone who keeps a diary knowing that there are books and methodology that say establishing a rule makes it easier to stick with something, but the more rules you set, the less freedom you have, and this can make keeping your diary feel troublesome.

The less rules the better, I say. If you write a single sentence on one day, that’s fine. Don’t feel obligated to write within 24 hours, either.

I’d like everyone to think about how the rules of the world are a collection of things not to do, whether it’s traffic rule, company rule, or even a law. Having rules limits freedom, so keeping them to a minimum makes it easier to write in your diary.

![Image 11](https://www.1101.com/store/techo/tpr/contents/images/4pxgotad8_1735117295965_q1_img_en.jpg)

Sometimes I’ll write a “Tomorrow Diary” in my Hobonichi 5-Year Techo. Anyone who keeps a diary on a regular basis knows this, but there are days when you feel like you can write forever, and there are days you can’t even pick up a pen. If I’m having a day when I’m ready to write a ton, I don’t just limit myself to today’s page. I’ll write in an entry for tomorrow, or go fill in three days’ worth.

We live our lives thinking about more than just one thing, so if you’ve got something that popped into your head that you want to write down, write it down in the following page before you forget about it. You can absolutely fill in a diary entry for tomorrow. Even if you’ve got a day when you can’t get yourself to write, you’ll have the advantage of having filled in a bunch of pages beforehand. (Laughs)

I don’t have a rule where I have to only fill my pages with things that have happened within the last 24 hours. It feels much lighter when I just think of it as a record I’ve written that day.

Nothing says a diary needs to specifically be a record of something, or have something every single day, or really be anything specific at all. If you think of something or have a time when you feel like you can write, just open the page and go for it.

### I try to write so I can easily

understand it at a later date,

but my entries end up so long

that I stop writing altogether.

Is there a way to depict things

clearly even if

I don’t have a gift for words?

![Image 13: 古賀さんの答え](https://www.1101.com/store/techo/tpr/contents/images/4pxgotad8_1734940677875_kogasan_answer_en.png)

![Image 14: I recommend writing to someone!](https://www.1101.com/store/techo/tpr/contents/images/4pxgotad8_1735022752650_q2_answer_pc_2.png)

I assume this is talking about trying to write things in an easy to understand way, just to have the order of things mess up and having a hard time writing well.

I have a writing method I recommend to anyone who struggles to write and organize information simultaneously. It involves imagining you’re having a conversation.

One example is the diary of Anne Frank, in which she addresses her entries to an imaginary friend named Kitty. She starts with “Dear Kitty,” and goes on to say what happened that day as if conversing with a friend.

Kitty is Anne’s way of addressing letters to herself as opposed to an actual companion. If you address someone else like that when writing, it makes it easier to be objective about yourself. If you treat it like a conversation, like if you were to say “This is what happened today. This is what this person said. This is what I had for lunch.” Telling a story to another person is a method that allows you to more easily organize information.

You should also feel free to write things colloquially in your entries, and not worry about sticking to a style that’s more proper in writing. Even just imagining you’re talking to someone can make it easier to write clearly.

So you can start your entries by addressing them to someone dear, like Anne did, or you can write them as if you would write a letter to or talk to someone as a way of building your writing ability.

Anne Frank was able to write truly interesting daily entries in her diary at 13 years old, so it’s worth it to at least give it a try.

### I end up writing

so many complaints

that I don’t even like

reading back on it later.

![Image 18: 古賀さんの答え](https://www.1101.com/store/techo/tpr/contents/images/4pxgotad8_1734940677875_kogasan_answer_en.png)

![Image 19: Follow up to show the problem  has been solved!](https://www.1101.com/store/techo/tpr/contents/images/4pxgotad8_1735022752648_q3_answer_pc_2.png)

I think one of the reasons we keep a diary is to put our complaints and negativity in the past.

I’ll write negative things in mine too, like “we didn’t get anywhere in that meeting,” or “I wish I hadn’t taken on this project.” The difference is, though, that those things will end up in the past.

Most of the concerns you have right now will clear up in a week. The Hobonichi Techo is made of paper, so when you write down a complaint, you can go back and write over it again. So if you keep a diary with a black pen, you can go back with a red pen and write over it. Sometimes I’ll go back to a previous page and strike through the text with a red pen and write “done.”

Maybe you can even get a stamp that you use to denote that an issue has been resolved. Being able to stamp over an entry about something you were worrying about last week would do wonders for self-confidence.

![Image 20](https://www.1101.com/store/techo/tpr/contents/images/4pxgotad8_1735117295968_q3_img_1_2.jpg)

It’s also a good idea to go back and write follow-ups to old entries. Let’s say you wrote “I have a crush on someone! It’s so exciting.” And six months later, you come back to the entry and add “I was wrong. They didn’t think anything of me.” That can make your diary more engaging.

It’s another preconception that once you write a journal entry, it has to stay that way. Tell yourself you can add onto an entry later on, and that freedom will make for richer entries to your techo or diary.

![Image 21: Q.4](https://www.1101.com/store/techo/tpr/contents/images/p6qg3j822_1734583886489_Q.4.png)

### I’m worried my family

will find my diary and read it,

so I can’t write my true feelings.

![Image 22: 古賀さんの答え](https://www.1101.com/store/techo/tpr/contents/images/4pxgotad8_1734940677875_kogasan_answer_en.png)

![Image 23: Set aside your preconceptions about diaries.](https://www.1101.com/store/techo/tpr/contents/images/4pxgotad8_1735022752646_q4_answer_pc_2.png)

I keep my Hobonichi 5-Year Techo at the office because I worry about someone looking through it at home, so I understand your feelings. Keeping it at work is one simple option, but this person is assuming that they can’t write their true feelings.

Diaries have this strange reputation of needing to be this thing where you need to write out your true thoughts, and bare your soul to show who you really are.

This kind of pressure tends to make journal entries take on a negative tone. If you don’t pressure yourself to write out the naked truth in your diary, you can feel more at ease.

You’ll be able to write more easily if you treat it like a blog and just write in contents that you’d be fine with anyone seeing. You could write something as simple as “I had really tasty zaru soba for lunch!” You could also expand on that.

![Image 24](https://www.1101.com/store/techo/tpr/contents/images/4pxgotad8_1735117295969_q4_img_en.jpg)

Don’t hype yourself up too much, and feel free to write as the kind of person you want to be. Either way, I think it’s helpful for people to set aside the assumption that we need to reach into our innermost depths and drudge out the muck to put into our diary pages.
